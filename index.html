San Francisco  ·  Drug Policies & Crime Data

Changes:
San Francisco's Drug Policies Worked, Until They Didn't

During the new century, San Francisco has adopted a new perspective towards drug crimes, gradually moving away from pure enforcement, towards treating drug use as a public health issue. For a long time numbers seemed to support that change, but something shifted in the last years. This is what the data shows.

Instead of a war on poverty, they got a war on drugs. Tupac Shakur  ·  "Changes" (1998)
Introduction

A City That Decided to Try Something Different

What the data measures, and the human story behind it.

For much of the 20th century, drug enforcement across the United States followed a very strict approach. The Boggs Act (1951) established mandatory prison sentences for drug-related offenses. In the 1970s, the War on Drugs intensified, having a special emphasis on law enforcement, leading to the creation of the DEA.[1] The appearance of new drugs such as crack cocaine in the 1980s and other synthetic substances led to even harsher policies [2].

By the early 2000s, a growing number of people living on the streets and consumption-related health crises prompted some cities to ask wheter the strict enforcement approach was actually useful for solving the problem. San Francisco was among those cities [3]. Gradually, and without abandoning enforcement entirely, San Francisco began considering alternatives. The idea was not to stop caring about drug crime, but to treat the person behind the offense differently. To focus on prevention and harm reduction rather than direct punishment.

The San Francisco Police Department's official records [4] capture drug offense incidents since 2003, allowing to see the recent evolution of drug offenses in the city, and to understand how the shift in policies affected them. Drug offenses in those records divide into two fundamental activities: Sales & Distribution, the supply side, and Street Possession & Personal Use, the demand side. These two categories do not behave the same way under the same policies. Their difference, visible in the data, is where the story becomes interesting.

Act I — The City-Wide Story
Figure 1

Twenty Years of Policy, Captured in Two Lines

Street possession and drug sales responded very differently to the same policy shifts.

From 2003 to the late 2010s, San Francisco's drug offense numbers told an encouraging story. Both possession and sales incidents declined steadily — a trend that coincided with a series of increasingly progressive policy interventions. Then COVID-19 hit, enforcement effectively paused, and a sharp recovery followed. By 2025, drug offenses were climbing back toward levels not seen in a decade.

The chart below traces this arc through eight distinct policy eras. The vertical markers show when each major intervention was introduced. Notice that possession and sales do not always move together — when they diverge, policy is rarely the only explanation.

The Eight Policy Eras

Each era represents a shift in how San Francisco approached drug enforcement. The data outcome row shows what the numbers actually showed in the years following each change.

2003 – 2005

Pre-Reform Baseline

High-enforcement era. Drug arrests were treated primarily as criminal matters, with limited diversion options. Both possession and sales volumes were at their peak for the dataset period.

Data outcome: Highest recorded possession levels — over 7,500 incidents annually.
2006 – 2008

Drug Court Expansion

San Francisco expanded its Drug Court programme, offering treatment-based alternatives to incarceration for non-violent drug offenders. Judges could divert eligible individuals into supervised rehabilitation.

Data outcome: Modest initial decline in possession; sales remained elevated.
2009 – 2010

Back on Track DA Programme

The District Attorney's office launched a first-offender diversion scheme. Non-violent drug offenders could avoid prosecution entirely by completing education, employment, and community service requirements.

Data outcome: Steeper decline begins. Both possession and sales fall through 2010–2011.
2011 – 2013

AB 109 Prison Realignment

California's Assembly Bill 109 shifted responsibility for low-level offenders from state prisons to county jails, reducing sentence lengths for many drug offenses. A significant structural change to who gets incarcerated and for how long.

Data outcome: Continued decline, though sales show a temporary uptick around 2013.
2014 – 2017

Prop 47 — Felony Reclassification

California voters passed Proposition 47, reclassifying simple drug possession from a felony to a misdemeanour. This was the single largest structural change to drug enforcement in California in decades, reducing arrest incentives significantly.

Data outcome: Sharpest sustained decline in possession. Sales also fell but more gradually.
2018 – Feb 2020

Safe Injection + LEAD Period

San Francisco introduced the Law Enforcement Assisted Diversion (LEAD) programme, redirecting drug users away from arrest toward social services. Simultaneously, safe injection site proposals advanced in city council debates.

Data outcome: Possession at historic lows. LEAD showed promise — before the pandemic disrupted everything.
Mar 2020 – 2021

COVID Enforcement Collapse

Pandemic lockdowns effectively suspended normal enforcement activity citywide. Police focus shifted, courts operated at reduced capacity, and outdoor drug activity became more visible as indoor spaces closed.

Data outcome: Recorded incidents hit the dataset minimum — but open drug use was widely reported to increase.
2022 – 2025

Post-COVID Resurgence

A sharp rebound in recorded drug offenses, particularly possession and opioid-related incidents, driven by fentanyl's dominance in the street drug supply and a return to active enforcement. The Tenderloin Emergency Declaration (2022) attempted to respond with both policing and services.

Data outcome: Fastest rate of increase in the dataset. 2025 figures approach pre-Prop 47 levels.

The city-wide trend is clear. But averages hide geography. The next act looks inside each of San Francisco's ten police districts to ask: did every neighbourhood experience the same story?

Act II — The Geographic Story
Figure 2

The Map That Moves With Policy

Month by month, district by district — where did the numbers actually change?

The interactive visualisation below shows two things simultaneously. On the left, the choropleth map colours each of San Francisco's ten police districts according to how their monthly drug offense count compares to the average of the previous policy era. Blue means improvement relative to what came before; red means worsening. The era badge at the top right updates as you move through time.

Through most of the 2003–2019 period, the map stays predominantly blue — a visual confirmation of what the city-wide chart showed. The critical moment comes around 2021–2022: district after district begins to turn red, and it does not recover. The post-COVID resurgence is not confined to one corner of the city; it is citywide.

On the right, the ten bar charts show the sales-to-possession ratio for each district across all eight policy eras. A ratio below 1 means personal use dominates — the district's drug problem is primarily one of addiction and harm reduction. A ratio above 1 means active dealing outpaces personal use — a supply-side problem that requires different interventions entirely. Watch how this balance shifts over time, particularly in the post-COVID era where several districts show sales activity rising faster than possession.

The geography tells us where drug crime has changed. But it does not tell us what kind of drug crime is driving the resurgence. That answer requires looking at the composition of offenses — and it reveals something unexpected.

Act III — The Composition Story
Figure 3

Not the Same Problem It Used to Be

The drugs have changed. The actions have changed. The policy response has not kept pace.

In 2003, San Francisco's drug problem was dominated by cocaine — specifically crack cocaine — and marijuana. By 2025, those drugs have largely faded from the official record, replaced by opiates and methamphetamine. This is not a story of more of the same; it is a story of a fundamentally different drug market emerging underneath the same policy framework.

The streamgraph below shows how the volume and composition of drug offense types shifted year by year. Toggle between Action Type (what the person was doing) and Drug Type (what substance was involved) to see both dimensions. The rise of opiates from near-zero before 2018 to one of the dominant categories by 2023 is among the most consequential changes in the dataset — and one for which San Francisco's harm-reduction infrastructure, designed largely around cocaine and heroin, was not prepared.

Conclusion

Progress Was Real. So Is the Reversal.

What the data tells us — and what it cannot.

The evidence across all three acts points to the same conclusion: San Francisco's progressive drug policies achieved measurable results. Possession incidents fell by more than 80% between 2009 and 2021. Sales declined across every district. The harm-reduction framework — diversion programmes, treatment courts, LEAD — was working, at least by the metrics that policing data can capture.

The post-COVID period has reversed much of that progress, and the reversal carries a warning that goes beyond simple enforcement failures. The drug supply has changed dramatically — fentanyl and synthetic opiates now dominate a market that the city's policy infrastructure was designed to address when heroin and crack cocaine were the primary concerns. A harm-reduction framework built for one drug crisis may be structurally ill-suited to another.

Data cannot answer what policy should come next. But it can make one thing clear: the problem returning to San Francisco's streets in 2025 is not the same problem that the city spent two decades learning to manage. Treating it as if it were is unlikely to produce different results.

"The data does not ask for optimism or pessimism. It asks for honesty about what has changed — and the courage to respond to what is actually there, not what was there before."
